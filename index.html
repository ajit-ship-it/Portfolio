Discovery Case Study · 2026

AutoPay & Account Funding for a credit-union platform.

Four clients. Twelve feature asks. One question: what do we actually build first? This is the discovery I led to answer it.

Role
UX Designer — sole designer on the discovery
Scope
Stakeholder discovery & prioritization
Methods
Stakeholder interviews, transcript synthesis, persona mapping, P0–P2 alignment
Outcome
Consolidated 12+ feature asks across 4 clients into a single priority roadmap the team could ship against

TL;DR

I ran discovery across four credit-union clients for a new AutoPay and Account Funding capability. The working assumption going in was that everyone wanted the same thing — they didn't.

The synthesis shifted the team's framing from "build every feature each client asked for" to "compliance and core sync first, growth features next, the legacy Express Pay rebuild last." Four clients aligned on a P0–P2 list within one working session.

01

Context

The product

A credit-union messaging and payments platform that helps community lenders engage members through SMS, email, and in-app channels. Two new capabilities were on deck:

  • Account Funding — letting members deposit into a savings or checking account they just opened, without needing to walk into a branch or call a service rep.
  • AutoPay — letting members set up recurring loan payments from a debit card, ACH, or digital wallet, without the credit union having to chase them every cycle.

Why discovery mattered

Four credit unions had asked for both capabilities — but each framed the problem differently. One client led with compliance; another with collections; another with growth. Without discovery, the engineering roadmap risked becoming a stack of one-off configurations instead of a coherent product.

My job was to figure out what was genuinely shared, what was client-specific, and what was a non-goal disguised as a priority.

02

Discovery approach

Four client conversations, captured as transcripts, then synthesized by theme, persona, and priority. I deliberately ran the synthesis after all four calls — not after each — to avoid anchoring on the first client's vocabulary.

  1. 1

    Listen broadly

    One discovery call per client (4 in total), framed as open-ended walkthroughs of their current AutoPay and Account Funding flows. No demo, no pitch — I wanted them describing their world, not reacting to ours.

  2. 2

    Tag every ask

    Pulled every feature request, complaint, compliance concern, and offhand worry into a flat list. Tagged each by client, product area (Account Funding / AutoPay / Express Pay), and signal strength (mentioned once vs. raised by multiple clients).

  3. 3

    Cluster into themes

    Surfaced four shared pain themes that crossed at least two clients. The rest stayed as client-specific notes — useful for account managers, but not roadmap drivers.

  4. 4

    Map personas, not people

    Each client had a dominant lens: compliance, growth, operations, or collections. The same feature ask meant different things depending on which lens framed it.

  5. 5

    Prioritize against the lens

    Built a single P0 → P2 list, with each item tagged by which persona raised it and which would block adoption if it shipped late.

A note on the four clients

For this public write-up, the four credit unions are referred to as Client A, B, C, and D. The findings, persona structure, and priority outputs are unchanged.

03

Four pain themes

The four themes that crossed at least two clients. Each one was a user-side problem, not a feature gap — which mattered for how I framed the recommendations later.

Theme 01

Slow funding is bleeding new members

Clients A and C described their current deposit process as entirely manual — members have to call in, walk into a branch, or physically deposit funds. There is no campaign-driven or automated way to prompt members to add money to a new account.

"It's all manual. Yeah, we don't have… it's a manual process." Stakeholder, Client A

Implication: The pain is felt at onboarding, not at servicing. The fix has to live where members already are — SMS, email — not behind another login.

Theme 02

Account funding is table stakes, not a differentiator

A stakeholder at Client A made it pointed: every modern onboarding flow already has account funding. Shipping it is table stakes; building it as a hero feature is the wrong frame.

"I think everybody is going to already have it for account opening. I wouldn't build it first because everybody has it. I don't think there'd be as much interest." Stakeholder, Client A

Implication: Don't market Account Funding as the headline. Build it well, ship it quietly, and let AutoPay and Express Pay carry the differentiation story.

Theme 03

High-friction funding kills account opening

Client C described drop-off at the funding step during account opening: limited to card and ACH only, no modern wallets, enough friction that applicants abandon. They can't complete funding, so they can't finish opening.

Their current funding methods are purely traditional — card and ACH only — because integrating Plaid through their loan origination platform is very expensive. Applicants used to modern money movement are dropping off. Synthesis from Client C call

Implication: The funding-method gap is a conversion problem disguised as a feature request. Wallet expansion (Apple Pay, Cash App, Venmo, PayPal) is the P1 lever.

Theme 04

Members are calling in for AutoPay reminders

Client C surfaced this most concretely: members are actively calling in just to be reminded and resent a payment link. There's no reliable, automatic way for them to stay on top of a due date.

"We're already getting members who are calling in. They're like, 'Hey, I got a text last month. Can I get a text again this month?'" Stakeholder, Client C

Implication: The AutoPay opportunity isn't a new payment rail — it's removing a recurring inbound call. Frame the pitch as deflection, not enrolment.

04

Four stakeholder personas

Same product. Four very different lenses. Naming the lens made disagreements legible — they weren't conflicting requirements, they were the same requirement read through different priorities.

Client A

Compliance-first

Top priority
Audit trail, NCUA-aligned consent, deposit campaigns for existing members
Tells
"We've already vetted this with Repay." Cares about regulator language before features.
Blocker if missed
SMS opt-in & consent capture in the audit log

Client B

Growth-focused

Top priority
Savings-account funding campaigns to lift balances
Tells
Reaction to AutoPay was "very well lined out." Already bought-in; wants to move fast.
Blocker if missed
Link-based campaigns + engagement analytics

Client C

Operations-focused

Top priority
Wallet-rich account-funding widget; deflecting inbound calls
Tells
Counts call volume per cycle. Frames every feature as a cost per member, per month.
Blocker if missed
Post-funding setup prompt + savings mandate

Client D

Collections-first

Top priority
AutoPay post-collections, with grace periods that match policy
Tells
Pre-launch, but has very specific rules per loan type. Will not adopt until per-product config is real.
Blocker if missed
Per-loan-type grace and minimum amount config

Where they aligned

  • All four want one platform for funding and deposit frictionless flows — they're tired of stitching providers.
  • All four cited manual effort or call volume as the business case, not "modernization."
  • All four would adopt if SMS, mobile web, and a hosted fallback are first-class — not afterthoughts.

Where they diverged

  • Client A wants to ship audit-trail and consent before any growth feature.
  • Client D wants per-loan-type config or it won't enable AutoPay at all.
  • Client C wants Apple Pay, Cash App, Venmo, PayPal in the wallet — Client A treats them as future scope.

05

Consolidated priority list

One list for the product team, owned by the design lens. Each item is tagged with the persona that raised it and the user problem it solves — not just the feature label.

Priority Feature area Action Raised by
P0 Compliance & consent NCUA verbiage, audit trail for AutoPay setup, SMS opt-in guidance Client A
P0 Core sync Real-time balance sync, auto-cancel AutoPay on loan payoff Clients C, D
P1 AutoPay date controls Grace-period config per loan type, member disclosure Client D
P1 AutoPay loan logic Per-method, per-context behaviour (ACH vs card) Clients C, D
P1 AutoPay re-access Generic management link + agent-side AutoPay cancel Clients C, D
P1 Wallet expansion Apple Pay, Cash App, Venmo, PayPal for cash-heavy adjacent members Clients C, D
P1 Account-funding campaigns SMS/email campaign flow for savings deposits to existing members Client B
P2 Express Pay revamp Hold until Q2 revamp; then re-engage Clients C and D Clients C, D
P2 Per loan-type config Min/max amounts, fee rules, grace periods per product type Client D

The hardest decision was Account Funding's placement. Three clients ranked it highly — but the framing in Theme 02 (Client A: "everybody has it") flagged it as table stakes. I held it as a P1 with a campaign-led entry point, not a hero feature. The product team agreed.

06

Design moves coming out of discovery

Lead with consent, not features

The first AutoPay screen should foreground SMS opt-in and the NCUA-aligned consent line. This is the unlock for Client A — without it, no other feature ships there.

Treat AutoPay as call-deflection

The success metric is not enrolment volume — it's the drop in "remind me again" inbound calls. Reminder cadence, fail states, and self-serve management are the design surface that matters.

Wallets as a conversion lever

Apple Pay, Cash App, Venmo, PayPal aren't a checkbox — they're the fix for the account-opening drop-off Client C named. Design wallet selection as the most prominent choice on the funding step, not a secondary tab.

Per-loan-type config as a first-class screen

Client D won't enable AutoPay without it; if it ships as a config-file edit, it ships as nothing. Build it as an admin screen with grace, min, max, and disclosure copy fields — not a JSON blob.

07

Reflection

What worked

  • Running synthesis after all four calls kept me from anchoring on the first client's vocabulary. The "table stakes" framing in Theme 02 would have looked like an outlier if I'd already grouped Account Funding as P0.
  • Naming the four personas by lens (compliance / growth / operations / collections) gave the engineering and PM partners a quick way to sanity-check decisions: "which lens is this for?"
  • Tagging each priority by which client would block adoption if missed turned the priority list from opinion into commitment.

What I'd do differently

  • Run a second, shorter call with each client after the synthesis, walking them through their assigned persona. I assigned them; I didn't validate the assignment.
  • Bring a member-side researcher in earlier. The four conversations were with credit-union staff — the member-facing pain is inferred, not directly observed.
  • Sketch the AutoPay reminder flow during the calls. With Theme 04 this clear, I could have started shaping the cadence and copy in the same session instead of a follow-up.

The single most useful artefact from this discovery wasn't the priority list — it was the persona lens. Six months from now, when a fifth client lands with a new feature ask, the team has a way to read the request without re-running the discovery.
